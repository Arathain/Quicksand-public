The game is centred around a 'hunt' paradigm, providing a more protracted and varied-intensity approach than a 'fight to the death', as seen with a traditional 'boss' battle. Throughout a single run, [[Beast]]s will generally transition between 'fight' and 'flight' states; this allows players time to recover in-between fights, and allows for a fight to last up to an hour (for harder challenges) without being a constant ramp-up of tension. 

The structure of a hunt can be described via a 'tension curve' - affected by monster difficulty, player situation/attrition, and environmental hazards, tension should **rise** throughout the run, with players being given momentary reprieves in the form of Beasts fleeing, retreating, or 'proceeding' in the case of a beast attempting to reach a personal objective. Upon run end, all of the built-up tension finally culminates with the players receiving their appropriate rewards/etc - this release will feel better the higher the tension at the end of a run. Some simple examples to illustrate the concept are provided below - it is important, however, to treat these as a conceptual tool: they are not a be-all-end-all solution, and mustn't be treated as a strict paradigm to be followed.

The simplest example of a *tension curve* possible; in the top-left is illustrated a 'break' provided by the flight of a monster, allowing tension to temporarily decrease before building up again. It is important to note that **tension builds up regardless of adding external factors**, simply by the nature of a time and health-limited environment. Such environmental factors, as well as any other 'ambient' inducers of tension, can and should be adjusted and adjusted for in the context of a given hunt.

An 'anticlimactic' curve, that drops off at the end; this will generally make the player feel less satisfied with the run - should be saved for enemies that the player should feel conflicted about fighting.
![[tension_curves_2.png]]

A more 'dramatic' curve, lending itself well to fights that finish explosively; a fight will feel more frantic and difficult if the ramp-up is purposefully muted to create sudden 'spikes' of tension. While 'tension curves' can theoretically be applied to any 'scale', such more extreme jumps are better suited to harder fights, in order to aid with how they're perceived by players.

Upon every time an enemy is 'wounded' via part break or simply fleeing, they can drop Materials and [[Relic]]s; materials serve as a source of metaprogression for [[Data Chips]] and [[Weapon Cores]], while Relics act as a means of providing progression throughout a run.
